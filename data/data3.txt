The fabrication of perovskite solar cells can be achieved using a variety of methods, including solution processing and vapor deposition techniques. Solution-based methods, such as spin coating, are widely used due to their simplicity and low cost. These methods allow for scalable manufacturing and compatibility with flexible substrates.

However, solution processing can introduce variability in film quality. Issues such as incomplete crystallization, pinhole formation, and non-uniform thickness can negatively affect device performance.

Vapor deposition methods, on the other hand, offer greater control over film uniformity and composition. These methods can produce high-quality films with fewer defects, but they are generally more expensive and complex.

There is often a trade-off between efficiency and stability. Highly efficient devices may use compositions that are less stable under environmental stress, while more stable formulations may sacrifice some performance.

Optimizing fabrication conditions, such as temperature, solvent choice, and annealing protocols, is critical for achieving a balance between efficiency, reproducibility, and long-term stability.
