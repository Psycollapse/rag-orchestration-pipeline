Perovskite materials have emerged as a promising class of semiconductors for photovoltaic applications due to their exceptional optoelectronic properties. These materials typically adopt a crystal structure of the form ABX3, where A is a cation such as methylammonium (MA) or formamidinium (FA), B is a metal cation such as lead (Pb), and X is a halide anion such as iodide (I), bromide (Br), or chloride (Cl).

One of the most important characteristics of perovskite materials is their tunable bandgap. By adjusting the composition of the halide ions, researchers can modify the bandgap to optimize light absorption across different regions of the solar spectrum. This tunability allows perovskite solar cells to achieve high power conversion efficiencies.

Perovskites also exhibit high absorption coefficients, meaning they can absorb a large portion of incident light within a very thin layer. This property enables the fabrication of lightweight and flexible solar cells, reducing material usage and manufacturing costs.

Another advantage is their long carrier diffusion lengths, which allow charge carriers (electrons and holes) to travel significant distances without recombining. This improves the efficiency of charge extraction in solar cells.

Despite these advantages, perovskite materials are known to be sensitive to environmental factors such as moisture, oxygen, and heat. This sensitivity poses challenges for long-term stability and commercialization.
