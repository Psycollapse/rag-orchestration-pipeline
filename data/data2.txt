One of the major challenges facing perovskite solar cells is their limited stability under real-world operating conditions. Degradation mechanisms are often associated with environmental exposure, particularly to moisture, oxygen, and thermal stress.

Moisture can penetrate the perovskite layer and cause chemical decomposition, leading to the formation of lead iodide and other byproducts. This process significantly reduces device performance. Oxygen can also contribute to degradation, especially under illumination, where photo-oxidative reactions occur.

Thermal instability is another concern. Elevated temperatures can accelerate ion migration within the perovskite lattice. Ion migration refers to the movement of ions such as iodide or methylammonium within the material, which can lead to hysteresis in device performance and long-term degradation.

Interface instability between the perovskite layer and charge transport layers also plays a critical role. Poor interface engineering can result in recombination losses and structural degradation over time.

To address these challenges, researchers are exploring strategies such as compositional engineering, encapsulation techniques, and the use of more stable inorganic cations.
