Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) is a technique used to improve the reliability of large language models by grounding their responses in external data.

Instead of relying only on the model's internal knowledge, RAG systems retrieve relevant information from a data source and provide that information as context to the model before generating a response.

A typical RAG pipeline consists of several steps. First, documents are ingested and split into smaller chunks. These chunks are then converted into vector embeddings, which represent their semantic meaning. The embeddings are stored in a vector database such as Pinecone.

When a user submits a query, the system converts the query into an embedding and retrieves the most similar chunks from the database. These chunks are then passed to the language model, which generates a response based on the retrieved context.

The quality of a RAG system depends heavily on chunking strategy, embedding quality, and retrieval parameters such as k, which determines how many chunks are returned.

If k is too low, relevant information may be missed. If k is too high, irrelevant information may be included, introducing noise into the system.

RAG systems are commonly used in applications such as knowledge assistants, internal documentation search, and domain-specific AI tools.
